Case Studies

In this project I worked with five other team members of the information architecture class at U of T to redesign the Information Architecture (IA) of Kijiji website based on User Centered Design and through utilizing IA principles and techniques.

Our group started the project by studying existing information architecture problems on the website. This helped us gauge the effectiveness of Kijiji’s current setup by not only researching the users that utilize their services, but also figuring out the pain points that have a high potential to pull users.

The main problems that we identified in our research were:

  • Poor classification, labelling and content placement
  • Inconsistency in the information layout and interface design
  • Unclear and inconsistent navigation
  • Poor bottom-up IA structure
  • Information overload

In order to address and resolve the problems identified above, our group conducted an analysis on the context, content and users in which I had the primary responsibility on the context analysis. It requires identifying areas for improvement in categorization and product classification, including proper labeling and better use of images and icons.

As for the content we adopted Noah’s Ark approach to analyze the content on Kijiji website and audited different web pages and summarized the main content of Kijiji into a table.

Then, we conducted a user survey by recruiting participants through convenience sampling and using google forms. We collected users’ responses about their past experience on Kijiji by asking 7 survey questions including their frequency of use, status of registration, transaction history, evaluation and experience with the free service and paid service options.

Our survey as well as the card-sorting exercises which we conducted along the way provided the most information in terms of categorization and classification issues, navigation, presentation of the website, as well as perspective on the user pain points. Majority of users expressed dissatisfaction concerning the main functionalities of the website, which affirms the need to further develop and improve the Information Architecture of Kijiji. From the card-sorting exercise we further found that it was essential for Kijiji to reorganize its category structure to provide better labelling of its products, services, and to introduce intuitive navigation for users. This was further affirmed by the survey responses.

By analysing the collected data from the above activities, we created use cases, scenarios, preliminary IA schematic diagram and low-fidelity design sketches (see the images)

Finally, we created a clickable medium-fidelity prototype which focuses on the IA of the Kijiji user interface. Our review and redesign of the four main information architecture pillars based on user research and design iteration helped to narrow down the pain points and key areas to focus on. Using the key design principles, we believe that the combination of our design suggestions should result in better usability and user experience for Kijiji’s user base.

In this project I worked with two other team members of the U of T iSchool to design a PRESTO/TTC payment system for a mobile user interface. Our design was presented for iOS platforms in iphone 6. However the strategy was to utilize responsive design as well as making gestures and interactions compatible with android phones and other operating systems.

The new Presto payment design focused on the following tasks:

  1. Signin/ signup: Customer signs in with their credentials or creates an account using their personal and payment information
  2. Loading/reloading passenger's card: Customers can either top up their Presto card manually or by enabling auto refill
  3. Retrieving passenger’s balance: Customers can view their balance as well as their payment history
  4. Fare payments (paying at the stations, streetcars or buses): Fares can be paid by holding the phone to the Presto machine or by tapping the pay icon

The design process was conducted in three phases:

  • Low-fidelity prototype using pen and paper
  • Medium-fidelity prototype using Balsamiq
  • High-fidelity prototype using Figma

In the first phase (low-fidelity prototype) we used sequential storyboarding method to show a visual story of a user experience sequence over time. All team members actively participated in the brainstorming session to come up with ideas based on existing systems as well as customer pinpoints such as payment criteria, GPS tracking, alerts, rate calculations and ID verification.

All members participated equally in the decision making process for selecting the key frames and providing annotations. I personally paid extra attention on inputs, buttons and overall layout consistency. Annotations were also added in the process to describe interactions during transitions.

In the second phase our group created used Balsamiq software to create a medium-fidelity prototype based on our sketches in the previous phase. Based on the feedbacks on the low-fidelity prototype we made some changes to improve the workflow and the UX. In this phase we used typography, images and wireframing templates and focused on IA, interactions and user flows. Our team decided to split the screens between all three members and merge them once they were completed and reviewed by all members. As for my individual task, I worked on the login, signup and user settings screen while reviewing and collaborating on other screens as well.

In the third phase we used Figma to create a high-fidelity prototype based on the screen mockups in the second phase. Our team mainly focus on two tasks (loading/reloading customer’s card and fare payments) according to the feedbacks we received in phase two to make a clickable prototype in high-fidelity.
